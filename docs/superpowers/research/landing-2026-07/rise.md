## 2. Section-by-Section Job Map

Since the original homepage is gone, I'm reconstructing from the surviving pages and the structure evident across their marketing materials. Their landing content was distributed across the homepage (now dead), `/calendar-for-project-management`, blog posts, and the App Store.

### Section A: Hero / Headline Block

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Job** | Instantly communicate the single value: you get more time for important work |
| **Mechanism** | Short declarative headline + subhead + CTA |
| **Headlines observed** | "Projects, tasks, time. Together." (site title) / "The calendar that works for you" (Medium launch) / "The smartest calendar in the world" (beta launch body) |
| **Subhead** | "helping you schedule, optimize and protect your day to give you more time for what's important" |
| **CTA** | "Get started. You are in charge." / "Get started today." |

**Notable:** The tagline underwent identity shifts: from "world's smartest calendar" (2022 fundraise) → "the calendar that works for you" (2023 beta) → "Projects, tasks, time. Together." (2024 pivot). Each reflects a strategic repositioning.

### Section B: Belief Statements / Manifesto Blocks

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Job** | Establish philosophical alignment — "we believe what you believe" — before showing features |

**Notable:** These do the job of making the scheduling automation feel *principled* rather than feature-listy. Each belief statement is followed by the specific feature that delivers on it.

## 3. Availability Copywriting Harvest

### Core Phrases (verbatim, annotated)

| # | Verbatim phrase | Source | What makes it work (or fail) |
|---|----------------|--------|------------------------------|
| 1 | "FocusGuard tracks how much time for deep work there's left and will automatically block off the remaining time if needed" | Medium launch | **Works.** Concrete mechanism: "tracks" → "blocks off" → "if needed." Shows intelligence without jargon. The conditional "if needed" avoids sounding aggressive. |
| 2 | "Automatic focus time blocking when you're close to being fully booked" | App Store | **Works.** Trigger-condition framing ("close to being fully booked") makes the automation feel responsive, not random. Students would resonate with "fully booked" as a stress signal. |
| 3 | "After enabling them they will silently help you have better days, without having to think about it anymore" | Medium launch | **Works.** "Silently" is the hero word. The promise is *absence of cognitive load*, not *presence of features*. |
| 4 | "Give us some wiggle room for some of your events, and we'll make sure they are optimized without needing your attention or approval" | Sunset retrospective | **Works.** "Wiggle room" is conversational. "Without needing your attention or approval" pre-empts the anxiety of automation. |
| 5 | "In Rise, you can turn static meetings into events that our Engine can move around to optimize for focus time" | Medium launch | **Partially works.** "Static meetings" → "events that move" is a nice contrast. But "our Engine" is cold/jargon-y. |
| 6 | "Flexible events will automatically resolve conflicts and improve focus time" | Medium launch (image caption) | **Works as caption copy.** Two promises in one sentence. "Resolve conflicts" is falsifiable. "Improve focus time" is measurable but vague on mechanism. |
| 7 | "Having more focus time blocks in your week feels…fantastic. Especially if you no longer have to think about it." | Medium launch | **Works.** Emotional payoff first ("feels fantastic"), mechanism second ("no longer have to think"). The ellipsis creates a pause that mimics the feeling of relief. |
| 8 | "Guarding your time is hard — there are always others that need you for something, and there are never enough hours in the day" | Medium launch | **Works as empathy opener.** Doesn't sell — acknowledges the problem. "There are always others" externalizes the enemy. |
| 9 | "We want to eliminate the need to think about systems for work, about focus time, about priorities" | Medium launch | **Works.** The repetition ("about X, about Y, about Z") builds cumulative weight. The verb "eliminate" is stronger than "reduce" or "help with." |
| 10 | "No Habit Change Required" | Medium launch | **Works exceptionally.** Three words, falsifiable, addresses the #1 objection to productivity tools. Could be a headline on its own. |
| 11 | "Features that would help optimize time autonomously" | Sunset retrospective | **Fails.** "Autonomously" is robotic. "Optimize time" is abstract. Compare to #3 — "silently help you have better days" says the same thing in human language. |
| 12 | "Automatic cross-calendar blocking to not run into double bookings ever again" | App Store | **Works.** Falsifiable ("ever again"), problem-specific ("double bookings"), mechanism clear ("cross-calendar blocking"). |
| 13 | "FocusGuard to dynamically protect your time" | Rise 2.0 blog | **Partial.** "Dynamically" is developer language that means nothing to users. "Protect your time" is good — frames time as an asset under threat. |
| 14 | "The calendar is finite. And that limitation? It's a feature, not a bug. It provides the boundaries we need to focus on what truly matters." | Sunset post | **Works philosophically.** Reframes constraint as design. Students who feel overwhelmed by infinite to-do lists would find this validating. |
| 15 | "It is one thing to help people have more time, but the second challenge comes right after: how do you spend that newly regained space?" | Sunset post | **Works as narrative bridge.** Acknowledges that freeing time is only half the job — spending it well is the other half. Directly relevant to Planevo's value prop. |

## 4. Falsifiable-Claims Inventory

Every testable, specific product claim Rise made on their public pages:

| # | Claim | Type | Source |
|---|-------|------|--------|
| 1 | "We have optimized thousands of hours" | Quantified outcome | Medium launch |
| 2 | "Considers thousands of possible solutions and figures out the best option" | Engine capability | Sunset post (TimeFold) |
| 3 | "Automatic cross-calendar blocking to not run into double bookings ever again" | Absolute prevention | App Store |
| 4 | "Flexible events will automatically resolve conflicts" | Functional guarantee | Medium launch |
| 5 | "FocusGuard tracks how much time for deep work there's left and will automatically block off the remaining time" | Conditional automation | Medium launch |
| 6 | "Rise uses hundreds of signals to find the best times to meet" | Specificity of inputs | Third-party description (Futur-IA) |
| 7 | "Scheduling links that highlight optimal times" | Feature existence | Rise 2.0 blog |
| 8 | "Tasks assigned to team members automatically show up in their calendars and update in real-time" | Sync guarantee | Project management page |
| 9 | "One billion knowledge workers are able to spend just 39% per week on their real work" | Statistical problem-framing | Medium launch |
| 10 | "Helped thousands of users with features like best time to meet" | User count | Rise 2.0 blog |

**Pattern:** Rise's strongest claims are conditional-automation promises ("when X happens, we do Y automatically"). Their weakest are vague quantifiers ("hundreds of signals"). The most defensible for a pre-launch product like Planevo: conditional-automation claims that can be demonstrated in product UI, not asserted with data you don't yet have.

## 5. Three Concrete Steals, Translated

### Steal 1: "No Habit Change Required" as a positioning line

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **The job** | Pre-empt the objection that all planners require discipline to work |
| **Rise's mechanism** | Three-word phrase used in body copy; backs it up with "flick of a switch" metaphor and stick-shift vs. automatic analogy |
| **Planevo translation** | **"Works without willpower."** Use as a subhead under the hero. Back it up with: "Dump everything on your plate. It becomes a calm board. Work gets placed into real free time on your calendar." The proof is the product flow itself — show the board auto-populating time blocks. For students: willpower is the scarce resource, not time-management knowledge. |
| **30% test** | Rise's phrase is "No Habit Change Required" — 4 words, workplace-coded. Planevo's is "Works without willpower" — 3 words, student-coded, different emotional register (discipline vs. executive function). Different backing proof (Rise: autonomous scheduling engine; Planevo: dump-and-reshuffle board). Passes. |

### Steal 2: Belief-statement section headers before features

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **The job** | Make the user feel philosophically aligned before you show them a feature list |
| **Rise's mechanism** | "We believe that everyone benefits from longer stretches of uninterrupted time" → then shows FocusGuard. Three beliefs, three features. |
| **Planevo translation** | Use two belief statements as section intros: **"Your calendar already knows when you're free. Your planner should too."** → then show the auto-scheduling visual. **"Plans that can't change aren't plans — they're guilt."** → then show the reshuffle animation. One belief per major section, not three (Planevo's page is shorter/simpler). |
| **30% test** | Rise uses belief-statement + feature reveal. Planevo uses the same structural pattern but with completely different beliefs (availability-awareness, anti-guilt) matched to completely different features (calendar-gap filling, reshuffle). The beliefs themselves are original to Planevo's thesis. Passes. |

### Steal 3: The "trigger-condition" framing for automation copy

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **The job** | Make automated scheduling feel intelligent and responsive rather than random or scary |
| **Rise's mechanism** | "when you're close to being fully booked" / "if needed" / "tracks how much time there's left" — always states the *trigger condition* before the action |
| **Planevo translation** | Every time we describe the auto-scheduler, state the trigger: **"When a class gets moved, your study block moves with it."** / **"When you finish something early, the next task slides forward."** / **"When life happens, the plan reshuffles — you don't."** This makes the automation legible and non-threatening for students who've been burned by rigid planners. |
| **30% test** | Rise's triggers are workplace-specific (overloaded, fully booked, double-booked). Planevo's triggers are student-specific (class moved, finished early, life happens). Same *grammatical* pattern (when X, then Y) but completely different semantic content and context. Passes. |

## 6. Three Deliberate DON'Ts

### DON'T 1: Do not use "world's smartest" / intelligence-bragging

**What Rise did:** "The smartest calendar in the world" — claimed intellectual superiority of the product.

**Why Planevo must not:** (a) It's unverifable marketing fluff that erodes trust, especially pre-launch with zero users. (b) Rise's own retrospective reveals this positioning didn't help retention — users "didn't notice Rise having a positive impact." Intelligence claims set expectations so high that any imperfection feels like betrayal. (c) Students don't want "smart" — they want *calm*. "Smart" implies the tool is cleverer than you, which is condescending to an audience already dealing with imposter syndrome.

### DON'T 2: Do not adopt a generous free tier to grow

**What Rise did:** Most features free, Pro at $16/month. Result: catastrophic — couldn't pay engineers even if all active users converted.

**Why Planevo must not:** Rise's post-mortem is an explicit case study in this failure mode. For a student product with inherently low willingness-to-pay, the free tier must be *useful but clearly limited* (e.g., one calendar, no reshuffle) so the upgrade has a visible unlock. Don't replicate Rise's "give everything away and hope teams pay" strategy.

### DON'T 3: Do not frame around "teams" / workplace collaboration

**What Rise did:** Nearly every piece of copy says "you and your team." The entire product was oriented toward getting multiple team members onto the platform for network effects.

**Why Planevo must not:** (a) Students plan solo. A roommate doesn't need to be on Planevo for it to work. (b) Team framing makes the product feel like Slack/Notion/Linear — "another tool the group has to adopt" — which is the exact friction that killed Rise's growth. (c) The "team" frame created a chicken-and-egg growth problem Rise never solved. Planevo's growth vector is individual: one student tells another because *their* life got calmer.

## 7. Headline/Slogan Raw Material

### Patterns observed in Rise's copy:

| Pattern | Example | Rhythm |
|---------|---------|--------|
| **[Noun], [noun], [noun]. Together.** | "Projects, tasks, time. Together." | Tricolon + one-word kicker |
| **The [noun] that [verb]s for you** | "The calendar that works for you" | Personification of tool |
| **More time for what's [adjective]** | "more time for what's important" | Indirect benefit (the time, not the tool) |
| **[Verb], [verb] and [verb] your day** | "schedule, optimize and protect your day" | Three-verb action sequence |
| **Without [gerund] about it** | "without having to think about it" | Absence-of-effort framing |
| **[Feature name] + what it does in one clause** | "FocusGuard tracks how much time…" | Named-thing + plain-English mechanism |

### Verb choices (frequency-ranked):
1. **Optimize** (most used but most abstract)
2. **Protect** (frames time as asset under threat)
3. **Block off** (physical/spatial metaphor)
4. **Resolve** (implies conflicts already exist)
5. **Track** (implies awareness, not action)
6. **Guard** (defensive, boundary-setting)
7. **Eliminate** (strongest, used sparingly)

### Planevo-ready headline candidates (inspired by, not copied from, Rise):

| Candidate | Job it does | Borrows from |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| "A plan that already knows when you're free." | Communicates calendar-awareness as the core differentiator | Rise's "the calendar that works for you" — personification of intelligence |
| "Dump everything. We'll find the time." | Lowers activation energy; promises the hard part is done for you | Rise's "without needing your attention or approval" |
| "When life happens, the plan reshuffles — you don't." | Demonstrates adaptability as emotional relief | Rise's trigger-condition pattern |
| "Works without willpower." | Addresses the #1 student planner objection | Rise's "No Habit Change Required" |
| "Your free time, filled. Your busy time, protected." | Parallel structure showing both sides of the value | Rise's protect/optimize duality |
| "Plans break. This one bends." | Positions against rigid planners | Rise's "flexible events" concept, translated to material metaphor |

### Rhythms worth stealing:
- **Ellipsis pause for emotional beats:** "Having more focus time blocks…feels fantastic." → Planevo version: "Seeing your week fill itself in…that's the feeling."
- **Conditional poetry:** "When [trigger], [calm result]" repeated 2-3 times as a section
- **Three-word falsifiable promises:** "No Habit Change Required" → "No willpower required" / "No Sunday night planning"

## Appendix: Rise's Fate as a Cautionary Tale

Rise Calendar's shutdown (March 2025) after 4+ years and €2.5M in funding offers Planevo two lessons:

1. **Feature-only moats die.** Rise's "smart scheduling" could be copied by Google Calendar (which shipped Focus Time in 2021). Planevo's moat must be *audience specificity* (students) + *emotional framing* (calm, not productivity) + *network effects within cohorts* (shared class schedules), not scheduling-engine sophistication.

2. **"Automation over discipline" is the right philosophy but the wrong pitch for conversion.** Rise proved that "it works without you thinking" is compelling as marketing but disastrous as retention — users didn't *notice* the value and churned. Planevo must make the automation *visible* (show the reshuffle happening, celebrate the plan adapting) even as it requires no effort. The magic must be seen to be believed.
